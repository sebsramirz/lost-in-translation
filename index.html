§ 01

Background

Generative AI is celebrated for efficiency and perceived neutrality, yet a growing body of research shows that it reproduces and amplifies existing inequalities. Trained on Western- and Sinocentric data, today's frontier models risk extracting Southern data only to feed it back as homogenized cultural outputs — what Couldry & Mejias call data colonialism, accelerated by image and language models. This study tests that claim empirically across two regions embedded in distinct hegemonic tech ecosystems.

§ 02

Question & Hypotheses

Research Question

To what extent do generative AI systems misrepresent or homogenize culturally specific content from Latin America and Southeast Asia — and how do regional data governance frameworks address it?

H1 · Output Distortion

Generative AI systems produce culturally distorted outputs when prompted with culturally specific content from Latin America and Southeast Asia, with variation between regions reflecting proximity to different hegemonic tech ecosystems.

H2 · Governance Gap

Existing regulatory frameworks in selected countries treat AI primarily as an economic issue, without addressing cultural representation in generative outputs.

§ 03

Methods

Identical prompts across three cultural dimensions were submitted in parallel to two frontier image-generation models. Outputs were coded on a 1–5 distortion scale against authentic reference material, with policy frameworks reviewed for cultural representation provisions.

§ 06

Findings

01

Distortion is patterned, not uniform.

All 24 outputs scored 3 or higher on the rubric, but error clusters predictably. Models default to globally legible clichés (Carnival, tuk-tuks, generic Catholicism) when local specificity is required.

02

Hegemonic proximity reduces error.

ChatGPT performs noticeably better on Latin America than on Southeast Asia; Byteplus shows the inverse pattern. Cultural fidelity tracks the geography of training data and the political economy of the lab behind the model.

03

Governance treats AI as economic, not cultural.

None of the four national frameworks reviewed contains binding language on cultural representation in generative outputs. The cultural dimension of AI policy remains largely undefended.

§ 07

Implications

  • ▸Policy. National AI strategies in the Global South should explicitly mandate cultural impact assessments for generative systems and require representation benchmarks before public-sector adoption.
  • ▸Practice. Region-led benchmarks — built by communities, not labs — are needed to evaluate models on local fidelity, not only on global tasks.
  • ▸Research. Future work should expand the corpus, test open-source and regional models, and integrate native-speaker coders for inter-rater reliability.
§ 08

Limitations

  • ▸Small N (24 outputs); single run per prompt with no temperature control.
  • ▸English-language prompts may themselves bias outputs toward Anglophone reference frames.
  • ▸Coder subjectivity in distortion scoring; no formal inter-rater reliability test.
  • ▸Policy review limited to publicly available, English/Spanish/Portuguese-translated documents.

§ 10

AI Usage

Generative AI was used in two clearly delimited ways, both integral to the methodology:

(1) As the object of study. ChatGPT and Byteplus were prompted with culturally specific prompts across three dimensions; all image outputs — including hallucinations — were preserved and coded.

(2) As an analytical aid. AI assisted with structuring policy notes and drafting visual materials. All AI-generated content was reviewed, verified, and edited by team members. No AI output was used as evidence without independent verification.
